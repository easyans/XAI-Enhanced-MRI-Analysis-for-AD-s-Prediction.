Progressive Nature:

Alzheimer's disease typically begins with mild symptoms, like slight forgetfulness, and progressively worsens over time. As the disease advances, individuals may experience significant memory loss, disorientation, mood and behavior changes, and difficulty speaking, swallowing, and walking. The progression of Alzheimer's can vary with some people living 4 to 8 years after diagnosis, though others may live as long as 20 years, depending on other health factors.

Causes and Risk Factors:

The exact causes of Alzheimer's are not fully understood, but it is believed to result from a combination of genetic, lifestyle, and environmental factors affecting the brain over time. The accumulation of amyloid plaques and tau tangles in the brain are the hallmark abnormalities linked with Alzheimer's. Risk factors include age, family history, and genetics, with certain genes like APOE-e4 being associated with the disease. Other possible influences include previous head injuries, heart health, and education level.

No Cure but Treatments Available:

Currently, there is no cure for Alzheimer's disease, but there are treatments available that can help manage symptoms. Medications such as cholinesterase inhibitors and memantine may help improve cognitive symptoms temporarily. Various non-drug approaches like cognitive stimulation, regular physical activity, social engagement, and nutritional changes are also recommended to manage symptoms and improve the quality of life. Ongoing research is focused on finding better ways to treat the disease, delay its onset, and prevent it from developing.
